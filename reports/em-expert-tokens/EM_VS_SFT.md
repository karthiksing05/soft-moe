# When does EM actually beat standard SFT? — many personas × few episodes each

## The honest gap this test closes

Across the balanced-data experiments, the EM *two-phase scheme* rarely beat naive **joint SFT** (both using
the expert token): knowledge `em_A ≈ em_B` to the decimal, convergence found all-Phase-A ≈ joint, and — most
tellingly — **freezing the expert tokens at *random* init and training only the backbone matched training the
tokens**. On balanced data the learned embedding barely mattered. The only clear EM-scheme win was cold-start
([COLDSTART_RESULTS](COLDSTART_RESULTS.md)), which used data *imbalance*.

The thesis's mechanism predicts EM should win specifically when the backbone is capable *in aggregate* but each
speaker is *individually* data-starved — **many personas, few episodes each**. In joint SFT each token then
gets too few gradient updates; EM's Phase B fits each embedding against the already-capable frozen backbone.
This test probes that directly.

## Setup

- **64-persona synthetic pool** (8 voices × 8 topic-lenses, generated by Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct; `gen_persona_pool.py`).
- **Qwen2.5-3B**, sweep **episodes-per-persona n ∈ {5, 15, 40}** at K=64 (`make_pool_cell.py` carves each cell).
- **Three arms** (compute-matched backbone): **joint SFT** (`--phase full`), **frozen-random** (`--phase
  backbone` only — tokens never trained, distinct random init), **EM two-phase** (backbone → tokens).
- Metric: mean held-out per-persona ppl on unseen questions.

*(A first pass at 0.5B was **inconclusive** — swap-ratio ≈ 1.0 in every cell, i.e. no method reached
persona-conditioning at all; the model was simply too weak for this many-persona regime. The 3B run below
reaches specialization and answers the question.)*

## Result

![when EM beats SFT](figs/emwin.png)

| n (episodes/persona) | joint SFT | frozen-random | **EM two-phase** | EM vs joint | EM vs frozen |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 40 (ample) | 6.49 | 6.55 | 6.68 | −2.9% | −2.0% |
| 15 | 22.12 | 22.92 | **20.49** | **+7.4%** | **+10.6%** |
| 5 (starved) | 730.7 | 672.4 | **589.3** | **+19.4%** | **+12.4%** |

## Findings — the hypothesis holds

1. **EM's advantage over standard (joint) SFT grows as episodes-per-persona shrink.** Tied/slightly-behind at
   n=40, **+7% at n=15, +19% at n=5.** This is exactly the low-data-per-expert regime the thesis targets:
   joint SFT under-trains each token; EM's Phase B fits it against the aggregate-trained backbone.
2. **"Frozen-random works just as well" is a *balanced-data* artifact.** At ample data (n=40) frozen ≈ learned;
   but at **low data the learned embedding beats frozen-random by +10–12%** — Phase B genuinely matters once
   there aren't enough examples for the backbone to route on an arbitrary anchor. This directly answers the
   earlier "the random frozen vectors worked just as well" observation: they do — *only when data is plentiful.*
3. **At ample data all three tie** (EM marginally worst), consistent with every prior balanced-data result:
   there the *token* is the active ingredient and the *alternation* is a wash.

## Honest caveats

- **The n=5 win is in a weak regime.** Absolute ppl is ~590 and swap-ratio only 1.11 — persona-conditioning is
  faint; the +19% is a comparison among poorly-fit models. **The n=15 cell (ppl 20.5, swap 1.14–1.20, +7%) is
  the cleaner positive** — real specialization *and* an EM win.
- **A total-data confound.** Lowering n at fixed K also lowers the *aggregate* data, so the backbone is weaker
  too. The direction matches the mechanism, but the clean isolation is a **fixed-total slice** (vary K and n
  with K·n constant) — the recommended follow-up.
- 64 *combinatorial* (subtly-different) personas, single seed, 3B synthetic. The thesis's advantage should be
  larger with a stronger pretrained backbone and thousands of *real* speakers.

## Bottom line

**The EM two-phase scheme does beat standard SFT — but specifically in the data-starved-per-expert regime the
thesis is about, and the advantage grows the more starved each expert is.** That is also exactly where
*learning* the embedding (vs a frozen random anchor) starts to pay off. On balanced/ample data the token is
what matters and the alternation is optional; **the alternation earns its keep in the long tail** — matching,
and now generalising beyond, the cold-start result.
